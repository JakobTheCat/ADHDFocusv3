Time blindness & progress framing
ADHD involves a poorly calibrated internal clock and impaired prospective memory (Barkley, 2011; Toplak et al., 2006). Countdown timers activate time-pressure stress — the prefrontal cortex registers scarcity, which can trigger the same threat response as a deadline, flooding working memory with cortisol and derailing focus. A forward-filling progress indicator uses spatial, progress-framed visual processing instead — a pathway that doesn't trigger threat detection. The slow breathing-pace pulse also provides mild physiological regulation. Tap the time display to see the number if you need it.

Ultradian performance cycles
The brain cycles through approximately 90-minute peaks and 20-minute troughs of cognitive performance throughout the day — the ultradian basic rest-activity cycle (BRAC), first described by Kleitman (1963) and later extended to cognitive performance by Rossi (1991). Your energy log builds a personal map of these cycles, so you can schedule demanding work during peaks and lighter tasks during troughs — working with your neurology rather than against it.

Phase 1 · Prime
What's the most interesting question inside this task?
Reframe your work as a puzzle. The ADHD attention system is interest-driven — curiosity is one of the only reliable triggers for sustained engagement that doesn't require willpower.
Interest-based nervous system
Dr. William Dodson's research describes ADHD as an interest-based nervous system. Unlike neurotypical brains that can engage through importance or reward alone, the ADHD brain requires interest, challenge, urgency, or passion. Curiosity priming generates the interest condition before starting. Pre-task question-setting also activates the brain's search mode, which maintains engagement during information-seeking and is far more durable than task-based motivation for ADHD.

Phase 2 · Lock in
Set your environment
Consistent pre-work rituals create conditioned context cues. Your brain learns that this specific setup predicts deep work, and begins shifting neurological state before you've even started.
40Hz binaural tone (headphones required)
Gamma binaural entrainment
Binaural beats present slightly different frequencies to each ear; the brain perceives the mathematical difference as a third tone. 40Hz is the gamma frequency band, associated with focused cognitive processing and dopaminergic activity. Research by Oster (1973) established the entrainment mechanism; Chaieb et al. (2015) and Beauchene et al. (2016) suggest gamma binaural beats may modestly improve sustained attention and working memory. Effect sizes are small — this is a supportive tool. Headphones are required because the stereo separation is the mechanism.
Phase 3 · On-ramp
10-minute commitment lock
Don't switch. Don't check anything. Flow probability rises sharply after 8–12 uninterrupted minutes — this is the hardest and most important part.
Flow state on-ramp
Csikszentmihalyi's flow research (1990) describes the transition into flow as requiring sustained initiation — attention must resist distraction despite low initial reward. For ADHD specifically, once engaged past this threshold, dopamine-driven reinforcement becomes self-sustaining (Solanto et al., 2010) — the task itself generates enough dopamine to maintain engagement. The lock reduces the window during which you're most vulnerable to task-switching before this self-sustaining phase begins.

What this is
Focus is an ADHD management tool built around actual neuroscience — not productivity advice designed for neurotypical brains. Most apps treat ADHD like a motivation or organisation problem. It isn't. ADHD involves real, measurable differences in dopamine signalling, executive function, time perception, and emotional regulation. Every feature here is designed around those differences specifically.
The science behind each feature is accessible in-app — tap any ⓘ button to read it. Understanding why a strategy works dramatically increases the chance you'll actually use it.
Features and the science behind them
🎯 The One Thing
One task dominates the screen. Everything else fades to near-invisible. This mimics the salience-filtering function of a healthy prefrontal cortex, which in ADHD fails to assign appropriate priority weight to tasks — causing everything to feel equally urgent or equally unimportant.
Barkley (1997): Executive functions and self-regulation. Castellanos & Proal (2012): Large-scale brain systems in ADHD.
⏱ Ambient Progress Timer
Time fills forward like a vessel — no countdown. The number is hidden by default (tap to reveal it). Countdown timers can trigger time-pressure stress and cortisol spikes; progress framing doesn't. The slow breathing-pace pulse provides mild physiological regulation while you work.
Barkley (2011): Time blindness in ADHD. Toplak, Dockstader & Tannock (2006): Timing and temporal processing in ADHD.
3️⃣ 3-2-1 Start Ritual
Initiation paralysis — the inability to start despite knowing what to do — is one of the most disabling ADHD symptoms. It's caused by the amygdala flagging the task as threatening, triggering avoidance. A countdown gives the prefrontal cortex a specific, low-stakes sub-task (count down) that bypasses the threat response and creates behavioural momentum into the main task.
Brown (2005): Attention Deficit Disorder: The unfocused mind. Shaw et al. (2014): Emotional dysregulation in ADHD.
🎲 Variable Reward System
Task completions trigger rewards at four tiers — common (60%), uncommon (28%), rare (10%), and a guaranteed jackpot every 15 completions. The unpredictability is the mechanism: the dopamine system responds more strongly to variable reinforcement schedules than predictable ones. The ADHD brain is especially sensitive to this due to chronically lower baseline dopamine tone.
Sagvolden et al. (2005): Altered reinforcement mechanisms in ADHD. Schultz (1997): Dopamine neurons and reward prediction error.
☕ Boring Task Mode
Temptation bundling pairs an aversive task with something mildly rewarding you only access during that task. This activates the mesolimbic dopamine pathway for tasks that wouldn't otherwise generate enough dopamine for comfortable engagement. The 2-minute option lowers the initiation bar to near zero by removing the threat signal that triggers avoidance.
Milkman, Minson & Volpp (2014): Holding the hunger games hostage. Volkow et al. (2011): Dopamine in reward motivation.
💧 Body Check Nudges
During hyperfocus, the ADHD brain suppresses interoceptive signals — hunger, thirst, and fatigue go unnoticed for hours. Dehydration alone impairs working memory and sustained attention. These nudges reactivate physical awareness before it becomes dysregulating.
Critchley & Garfinkel (2017): Interoception and human behaviour. Adan et al. (2019): Nutritional psychiatry and brain performance.
🟦 Regulation Toolkit
Box breathing, physiological sigh, and cold water exposure are three evidence-based techniques for interrupting an amygdala hijack — the state where emotional flooding prevents access to the prefrontal cortex. These work via the vagus nerve and parasympathetic nervous system. They are physiological interventions with measurable effects on heart rate variability and cortisol, not just calming advice.
Huberman et al. (2023): Brief structured respiration practices enhance mood (Cell Reports Medicine). Porges (2011): The polyvagal theory.
🧠 Hyperfocus Mode
A four-phase sequence: curiosity priming (generates interest-based engagement), ritual lock-in (creates conditioned context cues), 10-minute on-ramp (gets past the flow threshold), and a 90-minute deep work session aligned with ultradian performance cycles.
Csikszentmihalyi (1990): Flow. Kleitman (1963): Ultradian basic rest-activity cycle. Dodson (2016): ADHD and the interest-based nervous system.
⚡ Energy Log
After each session, you rate your energy — higher, same, or lower. Over time this reveals your personal ultradian pattern: when your brain naturally peaks and troughs. You can then schedule demanding work during peaks and lighter tasks during troughs — working with your neurology, not against it.
Rossi (1991): The 20-minute break. Kleitman (1963): Sleep and Wakefulness — the basic rest-activity cycle.
What this is not
This app is not a medical device, a diagnostic tool, or a replacement for professional treatment. ADHD is a complex neurodevelopmental condition. Medication, therapy (especially ADHD coaching and CBT), and environmental support remain the most effective interventions. This tool is designed to complement those — not replace them.
This is a prototype — your feedback shapes it

Temptation bundling & 2-minute rule
Temptation bundling (Milkman, 2014) pairs an aversive task with a mild reward, activating the mesolimbic dopamine pathway to compensate for the task's low intrinsic reward. The 2-minute commitment exploits a documented phenomenon: initiation is the highest-cost moment. Once started, completion drive often takes over naturally. Committing to only 2 minutes removes the amygdala's assessment that the task is too large or too painful to begin — bypassing avoidance entirely.
